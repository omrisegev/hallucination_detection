What is L-SML?

L-SML stands for Latent Spectral Meta-Learner. It is the algorithm we use to fuse our 16 spectral features of the token-entropy trace H(n) into a single hallucination score, without using any ground-truth labels.

It is based on two papers:
  • Parisi, Nadler, Kluger — PNAS 2014 (link): The Spectral Meta-Learner (SML). Under the assumption that classifiers make conditionally independent errors, the off-diagonal covariance matrix of binary ±1 classifier outputs has a rank-1 structure whose leading eigenvector encodes each classifier's balanced accuracy — without ever seeing labels. Weights are estimated from this eigenvector and used to produce a weighted vote.
  • Jaffé, Fetaya, Nadler — 2016 (link): The Latent SML (L-SML). Extends SML to handle groups of correlated (dependent) classifiers. Instead of filtering out correlated features and selecting a subset, it detects groups of dependent classifiers via spectral clustering, fuses within each group using SML to produce a virtual classifier, then fuses across groups using SML again. All features are retained.
In our setting, each "classifier" is one of our 16 spectral features, binarized at its empirical median to a ±1 prediction of whether the model's response is correct or not. No labels enter the fusion — they are only used externally to compute AUROC.

⚠ Note: the numbers below use our pre-correction supervised pipeline, which used labels to orient feature signs and select feature subsets. The corrected fully-unsupervised L-SML (Steps 105–110) produces numbers ~5–30pp lower. Phases 13 and 14 report the corrected method against EDIS and VC/SC on GPQA.
