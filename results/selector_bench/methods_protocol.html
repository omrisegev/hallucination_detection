Step 186-187 · label-free feature-subset selection

Methods, algorithms & experiment protocol

What each compared selector is, where it was published, what it assumes, and whether those assumptions hold on our data.

What this experiment is

The July-2026 meeting picked label-free, per-cell feature-subset selection as the thesis's new algorithmic contribution. The pipeline stays single-generation and unsupervised: from one generated answer we extract up to 46 scalar views of the entropy trace (the canonical pool), and a fusion step (continuous L-SML, or U-PCR) combines a subset of them into one hallucination score. Until now the subset was fixed (GOOD_5), found by a labeled grid search — prior knowledge. The question here: can an algorithm choose the subset per cell (model × dataset × temperature), from the unlabeled feature matrix alone, and match or beat the fixed label-derived subsets?

Pipeline with the new stage

entropy / logprob trace → 46 scalar features (fixed offline signs, z-scored) → ▷ NEW: label-free subset selector ◁ → same L-SML fusion (unchanged) → label-free anchor orientation (epr) → AUROC

Selectors receive an UnlabeledCell object that structurally has no labels field — label leakage is impossible by construction. Labels are used only to score the final AUROC, exactly as for every existing method.

The six label-free selector families

A1 — Residual-guided selection (repo-internal objectives)

No external paper — the fusion models' own structure-fit statistics, in the SML lineage (Parisi et al. 2014; Jaffe et al. 2015) and the U-PCR projection model. K-rules from random-matrix theory: Ahn & Horenstein (2013) eigenvalue-ratio; Kritchman & Nadler (2009) sequential Tracy–Widom rank test.

Pick the subset whose fusion-model fit is best: minimize the L-SML Eq-14 spectral residual (exhaustively from the sweep cache, or greedily), or the U-PCR projection residual; route between L-SML and U-PCR per cell by whichever residual is smaller; or keep GOOD_5 and swap only K (the cluster count) using the rank tests.

Assumes: a subset on which the fusion model fits its structural assumptions better will also detect hallucinations better (residual ↔ AUROC correlation).

assumptionvalidated on our data?
residual tracks AUROC within sizeRefuted — pre-registered admissibility criterion (median within-size Spearman ≤ −0.10 AND ≥60% negative cells) fails globally; weakly admissible only on repgrid (−0.109) and qa (−0.170). Same fate as the old ρ≥0.75 filter.
router: smaller residual picks the better fusion familyRefuted — NOT-USEFUL in every domain; a constant choice is at least as good.
K estimable from subset covariance spectrumPartially — estimators disagree with each other (KN subset-vs-pool agreement 2%) yet ALL land within ±0.2pp of GOOD_5: K is simply not a lever here.

Classical spectral FS — Laplacian Score / SPEC / MCFS

He, Cai & Niyogi, NIPS 2005 (Laplacian Score); Zhao & Liu, ICML 2007 (SPEC φ2); Cai, Zhang & He, KDD 2010 (MCFS).

Build a Gaussian kNN similarity graph over samples; rank each feature by how well it respects that graph: LS = degree-centred Laplacian Rayleigh quotient; SPEC = normalized-Laplacian quotient with the trivial eigendirection removed; MCFS = L1-regress the top spectral embeddings on the features, score = max |coefficient|.

Assumes: the discriminative signal varies smoothly along the sample manifold (cluster assumption): features aligned with graph structure are the informative ones.

assumptionvalidated on our data?
graph smoothness &harr; label relevanceRefuted on our cells — correct/incorrect answers do not form separated sample clusters in feature space; all three land at ≈0.70 (c46) / 0.61–0.63 (h16), below the kurtosis floor.
MCFS: discrete multi-cluster structureRefuted — unstable on weak-cluster data (documented); its Lasso needed a scale-free fix (eigenvector targets shrink as 1/√n) to produce non-zero scores at n≥1200 at all.

A2 — GroupFS: selection through group discovery (+ DUFS baseline)

Lifshitz, Lindenbaum, Mishne, Meir & Benisty, “Unsupervised Feature Selection Through Group Discovery”, AAAI 2026 (arXiv:2511.09166). No official code — clean-room torch-CPU reimplementation grounded in the fetched paper; predecessor DUFS (Lindenbaum et al., 2021, Gated Laplacian) reproduced as the same-family baseline a2.dufs.

Jointly discover latent groups of related features and gate whole groups on/off. Three-term loss: sample-graph diffusion smoothness of the gated data + feature-graph smoothness of the group embedding (with orthogonality) + group-sparsity via stochastic gates; Gumbel-Softmax feature→group assignment, warm-started from spectral clustering of the feature graph. Selection = features of the open-gate groups. Documented deviation: under our CPU budget the paper's joint group-gate training saturates (all gates open), so selection pressure comes from per-feature DUFS gates aggregated to group granularity; discovery mechanics kept.

Assumes: informative structure lives in correlated feature groups; data varies smoothly on a sample manifold; group sparsity separates signal groups from nuisance.

assumptionvalidated on our data?
feature groups exist and are discoverableYes — planted-groups smoke: ARI ≥ 0.6, noise excluded; on real cells it finds a stable 8-feature core + cell-dependent periphery.
its groups improve L-SML's clustering stepPartially — feeding discovered groups to L-SML (a2.select+groups 0.7282) ≈ letting L-SML cluster alone (0.7323): clustering is not the bottleneck.
selection transfers to detection AUROCYes on the 46-view pool: 0.7323 vs GOOD_5 0.7328 label-free (10W/2T/7L per cell); Refuted on the already-curated H16 pool (0.6337) — nothing left to select there.

A3 — Concrete Autoencoder

Balın, Abid & Zou, “Concrete Autoencoders: Differentiable Feature Selection and Reconstruction”, ICML 2019 (arXiv:1901.09346). Torch-CPU reimplementation, linear decoder.

A concrete (Gumbel-Softmax) selector layer with k slots picks k features; a decoder reconstructs ALL features from them; temperature annealed 10 → 0.01; best-of-3-seeds by held-out reconstruction MSE + a closed-form greedy swap polish on the same objective; k from the val-MSE elbow.

Assumes: features that best linearly reconstruct the whole matrix are the informative ones (reconstruction ≈ relevance).

assumptionvalidated on our data?
reconstruction &asymp; label relevanceRefuted — the Step-151 negative replicated at scale: best 0.7099 (c46) / 0.62 (h16). Independently confirmed by A4's greedy CSSP on the same objective: identical h16 macro (0.6124 = 0.6124 at k=5), chosen-set Jaccard 0.52, per-cell r=0.85 — the objective, not the optimizer, is the dead end.

A4 — Antigravity track (anchor-affinity / intrinsic-dim / greedy CSSP)

No single paper — three heuristics built on the shared harness in the parallel Antigravity worktree (uncommitted branch selector/a4-antigravity-unsupervised). CSSP = classic column subset selection; rank rules reuse the A1 estimators.

(i) a4.anchor_*: rank features by |Spearman| with the epr anchor, keep the top-s (self-supervision by proxy). (ii) a4.intrinsic_k_ah: subset size = eigenvalue-ratio intrinsic dimension + 3, same anchor ranking. (iii) a4.recon_*: greedy forward selection minimizing linear reconstruction error of the full matrix (deterministic CSSP analog of A3). Plus GOOD_5 with K forced from the full-pool spectrum.

Assumes: features that agree with the strongest single view make a good ensemble; the covariance spectrum's effective rank prescribes the subset size.

assumptionvalidated on our data?
anchor-correlates form a good ensembleRefuted — it selects the anchor's clones (maximum redundancy): a4.anchor_s4 0.6593 ≈ epr alone 0.6606 (25W/26L per cell). The ensemble adds nothing over its anchor; GOOD_5's +1.1pp comes from complementary features this objective cannot find. Best learned selector on H16 nonetheless.
reconstruction &asymp; relevance (CSSP)Refuted — converges exactly to A3 (see above); confirms the objective-level refutation with a 100× cheaper optimizer.
smoke gateYes — 13/13 known-answer tests pass in the worktree; deterministic; zero fallback rows.

Simple-stats floors

No paper — the mandatory guardrails: random subsets, |kurtosis|, MAD, greedy decorrelation. (Random-floor discipline motivated by Rajabinasab et al. 2026.)

Rank features by a one-line statistic of the unlabeled column and take the top-s. Any learned selector that cannot beat these is not learning anything about the data.

Assumes: none (that is the point).

assumptionvalidated on our data?
learned methods clear the dumb floorPartially — embarrassingly, |kurtosis| (top-6) scores 0.7199 on the 46-view grid, above every classical-FS method and A3/A4; only the GroupFS family and the K-rule ties clear it.

The clustering-swap sub-experiment (from the theorem-validation lead)

The L-SML theorem validation found the Lemma-4 within-vs-between-cluster correlation contrast never materialized (negative in 4/4 inspected cells), so the bench folded in three ways to replace or override L-SML's internal spectral clustering: forced group assignments from GroupFS (a2.select+groups, a2.groups@good5), forced K from the two rank tests (*.good5+K_ah, *.good5+K_kn — both subset-spectrum and full-pool-spectrum variants exist), and the residual router between L-SML and U-PCR. Verdict: every variant lands within ±0.6pp of its untouched counterpart — two implementations, opposite K estimates, same answer: the clustering step is not where performance is lost, and the missing theorem contrast has no practical cost on this grid.

Objective admissibility (the pre-registered analysis behind A1)

Before trusting any selector that optimizes a label-free objective, we measured whether each candidate objective even correlates with AUROC among same-size subsets (Spearman, within cell × size, over the 65k-subset sweep; criterion fixed in advance: median ≤ −0.10 and ≥60% of units negative).

Consequence: no label-free objective is globally admissible — selection cannot be reduced to optimizing any single one of these statistics. The methods that work (GroupFS) do not optimize a fit statistic; they exploit structure (groups + smoothness + sparsity).

Verification discipline

Every new component passed a standalone known-answer unit test on synthetic data with an obvious expected result before integration (scripts/smoke_selectors.py, 17 tests: planted-K rank tests, fusion regression against pre-refactor fixtures, planted-group ARI, planted-factor recovery, mask round-trips, lookup-vs-live ≤1e−6, no-label-leak assertion). Gate order mirrored the cluster rule: component smoke → 2-cell bench → full bench.
