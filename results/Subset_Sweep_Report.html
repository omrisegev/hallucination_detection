Exhaustive L-SML Feature-Subset Sweep

0 — What was run, step by step

The question. We have 16 features extracted from every answer's token-entropy trace H(n), and the L-SML method fuses a chosen subset of them into one hallucination score. Until now the subset (GOOD_5) was chosen by intuition + small experiments. This sweep asks: over ALL possible subsets, which ones actually work, how does L-SML cluster and weight them, and can any of the other signals we ever built (BOCPD, HMM, Kalman, spilled energy, anomaly scorers) improve the fusion?

Step A — Stage 0: build the extra signals (scripts/build_derived_views.py). Why: the pivot-pilot signals are not in the cached feature files. What was done: (1) six anomaly scorers (Mahalanobis, GMM, KDE, IsolationForest, AE, PRAE) were computed from each cell's own feature matrix — possible for all cells; (2) for the 3 cells that have raw token traces on disk, the temporal signals were computed per trace: BOCPD change-point statistics on H(n) and on the logprob trace ΔE(n) (bocpd_ecp_spilled — answering "can we run BOCPD on the logprobs?": yes), a 2-state HMM, AR and Kalman innovations, and the 4 spilled-energy features.

Step B — the exhaustive sweep (scripts/run_subset_sweep.py). For every cell (a dataset×model combination, 51 total) and for EVERY subset of its usable features of size 3 up to all of them — 65,399 subsets when all 16 features are usable, ~1.9M subset-fits overall — we ran the full continuous L-SML pipeline: orient each feature by its fixed offline sign, z-score, detect dependent groups (spectral clustering, paper residual criterion), fuse within groups then across groups. Per subset we recorded: the AUROC, whether the label-free anchor had to flip the global sign, the number of clusters K and which feature sits in which cluster, the effective weight of every feature (within-group weight × its cluster's cross-weight — these reproduce the fused score exactly), and the correlations inside the subset. Honesty rules: the global sign is fixed by correlating with oriented epr (never with labels), and AUROC is reported raw — no max(auc, 1−auc).

Step C — augmentation stage. The extra Stage-A signals were NOT thrown into the exhaustive enumeration (that would multiply the cost ~64×). Instead, each extra view v was tested the way you would actually use it: add it to a good subset. For every cell we evaluated S∪{v} for S = GOOD_5, STABLE_H9, ALL_16 and the cell's top-20 subsets, with a paired bootstrap on the AUROC difference. Found: every extra view lowers AUROC on average (section 10) — the Step-151 "17th view" idea is closed.

Step D — the analyses over the sweep output. (1) Top subsets (section 2/2b) — but a warning first: picking the best of 65,399 subsets by test AUROC is massive selection bias (~+8.5pp, we measured it). So (2) LOCO (section 5) is the honest version: for each held-out cell, choose the subset on the other 50 cells, then evaluate it on the held-out cell. Found: LOCO cannot beat GOOD_5 — the current 5-feature choice is already at the honest optimum. (3) Feature marginal value (section 4): which features earn their place. (4) K-census (section 6): what the clustering actually does. (5) ρ-check (section 7): is the old "skip subsets with correlated pairs" rule justified? Found: no — high-ρ subsets score HIGHER; the clustering absorbs the dependence. (6) Method grid (section 9): L-SML vs flat SML vs simple average vs U-PCR on the same subsets. (7) Competitors (section 11): recorded SE / SelfCheckGPT / VC / LapEigvals numbers next to our label-free numbers.

Reading aids: a "cell" = dataset×model; "K" = number of dependent-feature clusters L-SML detected; "flipped" = the label-free anchor negated the fused score; weights are L1-normalized and signed. Full per-subset data: results/subset_sweep/*.npz; every table here also exists as a CSV.
