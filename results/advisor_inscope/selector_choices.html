The selector loses to the fixed GOOD_6 subset on 16 of 25 cells. This page answers the obvious follow-up: what did it actually choose, how does that differ from GOOD_6, and why does it choose that?

1. What it selects

GOOD_6 is six hand-curated views. The selector picks 19 views on average out of a ~28-view pool — roughly three times larger. It generally keeps most of GOOD_6 and adds a large tail around it.

3. Why it chooses that — the objective is aimed elsewhere

The gates are trained on a Laplacian-smoothness objective over the sample graph. That objective never sees a label, and it is not a proxy for separability. Measured directly: the rank correlation between a view's gate value (what the objective maximises) and that view's own oriented AUROC (what we actually want) is ρ = +0.149 on average across the 25 cells.

So the selector is not picking badly — it is picking for a different criterion. It does tilt in the right direction: the views it selects average 55.3% AUROC against 47.7% for the views it leaves out (+7.7 pp). But a ρ of +0.149 is far too weak to reconstruct a six-view subset that took a corpus-wide labelled search to find.

And that is the honest asymmetry. GOOD_6 was chosen once by macro AUROC across the whole grid, so it carries label information at the corpus level even though applying it to a new cell needs no labels. The selector is given no such prior: it must rediscover a good subset per cell, from unlabelled data, using an objective only weakly aligned with the target. Losing by ~2 pp under those conditions is a reasonable outcome, not a defect.

“Inverted-polarity” is not the same as “bad”, and the distinction changes the conclusion. A view with AUROC 0.30 is strongly informative — flipped, it scores 0.70 — and continuous L-SML flips it automatically with a negative weight. Of the below-chance views the selector actually picks, 80% sit below 0.40 (strong once flipped) and only 6% are genuinely near-random.

Splitting the two separates the win and loss cells cleanly: the cells it wins on take 8.1 strongly informative inverted views and 0.00 near-random ones; the cells it loses on take 4.8 strong ones but let 1.12 near-random views in. So the useful statement is the narrow one: admitting genuinely uninformative views tracks the losses; admitting inverted-polarity views does not. That is a direct argument for pruning the pool on uninformativeness rather than on raw AUROC sign.
