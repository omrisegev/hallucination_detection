GroupFS is a learned, label-free feature selector: it puts a differentiable gate on each view and trains them against a Laplacian-smoothness objective, discovering groups of views as it goes. We use it as a pre-fusion selection stage — select, then fuse the selected views with the same L-SML fusion the fixed subsets use.

It ships here with two selection rules over identical gates and an identical objective. a2.select opens whole groups (a group is in if its median member gate is open); a2.dufs thresholds each gate individually.

But saturation is not why it loses. The standing explanation for GroupFS's worst misses was that it opens nearly the whole pool and swamps the fusion. That is testable, and it fails: the rank correlation between the fraction of the pool selected and the per-cell gap to GOOD_5 is -0.028 — no relationship at all. Switching to per-feature gating eliminates saturation completely and moves the macro by just +0.20 pp, still below GOOD_5.

None of these reaches a level that would let us claim a mechanism. Read together with the selection-headroom result — that two thirds of any apparent subset-search gain is winner's curse, driven by sample size — the coherent reading is that label-free subset selection on these cells is dominated by selection noise rather than by a fixable defect in the selector. That is an argument against investing in another selector variant, not for one.

One cell is an outlier and deserves naming rather than averaging. inside_coqa_llama7b misses GOOD_5 by 18.0 pp under group-granular gating and 16.2 pp under per-feature gating. It combines the most extreme class imbalance in the set (positive rate 0.132) with 12 anti-oriented views among those chosen. It is the one cell where the “bad views got in” story is visibly true, and it is doing a lot of work in any macro average.
