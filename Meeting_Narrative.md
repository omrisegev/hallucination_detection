## The Core Idea

The thesis explores whether **Nadler spectral fusion** can improve hallucination detection in LLMs by aggregating multiple uncertainty signals ("views") of the same question.

The appeal of Nadler is that it is **unsupervised**: it estimates each view's reliability from the covariance structure of the signals alone, without needing ground-truth labels. This makes it realistic for deployment.

The hypothesis: if you present the same question to the model in K different ways and fuse the resulting uncertainty scores, the ensemble should outperform any single view — because different phrasings expose different failure modes, and Nadler can learn to weight the more reliable ones automatically.

---

## Two Uncertainty Measures, Same Fusion Strategy

We tested this idea with two different uncertainty measures:

- **Semantic Entropy (SE)** — samples multiple stochastic answers, clusters them by meaning using NLI, and measures entropy over the semantic cluster distribution (Farquhar et al., Nature 2024)
- **EPR (Entropy Production Rate)** — computes the mean token-level entropy of a single greedy generation; a lightweight, single-pass measure (artefactual library)

Both were wrapped with the same Jaffe-Nadler spectral fusion algorithm.

---

## Experiment 1 — Semantic Entropy with Diverse Views

**Model**: Llama-based  
**Datasets**: TriviaQA, SQuAD  
**Views**: Original SE + Formal rephrasing + Simple rephrasing + French translation + German translation + Verification pass ("Are you sure?") + Skeptic pass ("Does this contain errors?")

**Results**:
| Dataset | Baseline SE | Spectral Ensemble | Lift |
|---------|------------|-------------------|------|
| TriviaQA | 0.769 | 0.818 | **+6%** |
| SQuAD | 0.746 | 0.774 | **+4%** |

The ensemble consistently improved over the single-view baseline on both datasets. These results were encouraging enough to motivate extending the approach to EPR.

**Notable**: The Verify and Skeptic views are behaviorally different from the linguistic views — they ask the model to reflect on its own answer rather than rephrase the question. They measure *logical consistency* rather than *generation entropy*.

## Experiment 2 — EPR with Prompt Template Views

**Models**: Ministral-8B, Falcon-3-10B, Phi-4  
**Datasets**: TriviaQA (200 samples), WebQuestions (200 samples)  
**Judge**: Qwen2.5-7B as LLM-as-a-Judge for correctness labels  
**Setup**: Full float16 precision (matching the EPR paper), top-k=50 sampling

We first replicated the EPR paper's single-view results. Our numbers are competitive:

| Model | Paper AUC | Our AUC |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Ministral-8B | 81.4 | 75.1 |
| Falcon-3-10B | 75.4 | **80.8** |
| Phi-4 | 78.2 | 73.4 |

Then we added views. We tried three generations of view design:

**V1 — Multilingual** (formal/simple/German/French, generated by the model itself):  
Negative lift across all models and datasets. Language shift corrupts EPR because token distributions change when the question is in a different language.

**V2 — English prompt templates** (formal, factual, one-word, expert, direct, confident...):  
Still negative lift. Replacing translation with instruction variants removed the language problem but didn't solve the underlying issue.

**V3 — Optimized English templates** (from exhaustive search over ~11 candidates):  
Best combination found: `['direct', 'stepwise', 'paraphrase', 'concise']`, fusion AUC = 0.935 on 30 samples. But on the full 200-sample evaluation: still negative lift (−1.1 to −7.5 AUC points across all 6 model×dataset combinations).

**Summary of EPR fusion results (v3, 200 samples)**:
| Dataset | Model | Single-view EPR | Nadler Lift |
|---------|-------|----------------|-------------|
| TriviaQA | Ministral-8B | 75.1 | −2.2 |
| TriviaQA | Falcon-3-10B | 80.8 | −5.7 |
| TriviaQA | Phi-4 | 73.4 | −3.1 |
| WebQuestions | Ministral-8B | 68.9 | −7.5 |
| WebQuestions | Falcon-3-10B | 68.1 | −1.4 |
| WebQuestions | Phi-4 | 62.9 | −1.1 |

## Experiment 3 — View Optimizer: Systematic Search for Better Templates

To rule out "we just haven't found the right prompts yet," we built a separate agentic pipeline (`View_Optimizer.ipynb`) that:

1. Proposes new prompt templates using an LLM (Gemini → Claude → HF Qwen, with instant failover)
2. Evaluates each on 30 questions using EPR (with gold-answer string matching as labels)
3. Selects the best K=4 using a greedy diversity algorithm
4. Iterates for up to 6 rounds or until convergence

We also built an exhaustive C(M,4) search over all evaluated views, and replaced the selection heuristic with **DPP MAP** (Determinantal Point Process), which maximizes the log-determinant of a Spearman-kernel quality-diversity matrix. This has a theoretical 1−1/e optimality guarantee and uses the full continuous EPR distributions rather than binarised predictions.

**Finding**: DPP MAP tied with the naive disagreement heuristic. The exhaustive optimum was marginally above seed. The optimizer made zero improvement on several runs. Changing the selection algorithm had no effect.

## The Central Observation

The SE experiment produced positive lift. The EPR experiments consistently produced negative lift. What's different?

**One possible explanation**: In the SE experiment, the Verify and Skeptic views are a *different type of signal* — they measure whether the model maintains its answer under challenge, not how uncertain the token probabilities are. This behavioral dimension may be genuinely less correlated with entropy than any rephrasing can be.

All EPR prompt-template variants, regardless of how they are worded, pass through the same generation process and produce EPR scores that reflect the same underlying parametric knowledge. The correlation between them may be irreducibly high because they share the same belief state.

Nadler's conditional independence assumption — that views make errors independently given the true label — may simply not hold for prompt variations of the same model. When the model doesn't know a fact, it doesn't know it regardless of how the question is phrased.

**This is a possible explanation, not a confirmed conclusion.** We have not formally measured the pairwise correlation between EPR signals across views, and the SE positive result could also be partly driven by SE's richer sampling-based nature (multiple generations vs. a single EPR pass).

## Constraints

- **Unsupervised or self-supervised**: no ground-truth labels at inference time; self-supervised (using model's own outputs as pseudo-labels) is acceptable
- **Gray-box**: access to log probabilities, hidden states, attention maps — but no fine-tuning
- **Single pretrained model** (primarily Falcon-3-10B; also tested Ministral-8B and Phi-4)
- **Practical**: runs on a single A100 in Colab, ~10s per forward pass

## Open Questions

1. **Why did SE work but EPR didn't?** Is it the signal-type diversity (behavioral + entropic), the richer sampling nature of SE, or something else? Can we isolate the factor?

2. **What makes a good view for Nadler?** The theory requires conditional independence. What types of signals are most likely to satisfy this in practice for LLM uncertainty?

3. **What other signal types are accessible in the gray-box, unsupervised/self-supervised setting** that might be genuinely decorrelated from token-level entropy?

   Some directions we've considered:
   - Temperature-varied EPR (low T=0.3 vs high T=1.5 measure different uncertainty components)
   - Attention entropy at mid-layer (coherence of the attention graph, different modality from token probs, zero extra cost via forward hook)
   - Self-behavioral consistency (e.g. challenging the model's own answer — extending the SE notebook's Skeptic/Verify idea to the EPR setting)
   - Self-supervised OOD detection on hidden states (using EPR as a pseudo-label to define a reference distribution, then measuring distance from it)

4. **Is Nadler the right fusion method for this setting**, or is there a better unsupervised aggregation strategy when the number of views is small (K=4) and the views may not be fully independent?
